**My bullet points**
 
- A trend has started to emerge of using low-cost microphones designed for general 'bioacoustics' applications in experiments reporting to record acoustic emissions from plants
- These are short transient mechanical vibrations within the vasculature of higher plants, that occur during evaporation and transpiration. They were hypothesised early in the 20th C, first recorded in the 60s, and have largely been recorded using proprietary, commercial equipment from the civil engineering industry, where they are used to monitor cracking in building materials
- The recent articles have spawned a large number of qualitative review articles on how we view plant perception, but none that address the fact that, in stark comparison to the previous generation of studies, which used highly standardised equipment, all of these recent studies use completely different methods to collect and analyse these recording, some with very different microphone types, and in very different situations
- Given the wide variety of situations and the flexibility with which these recordings can be made, it becomes much more important to make the analysis traceable and reproducible
- To that end, we have collected a large amount of recording data, some synthetic, and some from plants, and tested each of the methods in turn, on each dataset, as well as on existing data. The result is that we can show that: 1) a combination of multiple microphones and statistical thresholds can be used to detect acoustic emissions from plants more reliably and accurately, as well as at higher noise levels; 2) both barley and rush plants do not produce many acoustic events when placed in an anechoic chamber

"Recent studies have revealed that plants emit ultrasonic airborne sounds during stress, particularly under drought and physical damage. These acoustic emissions provide a promising non-invasive window into plant biomechanical responses and physiological state. However, the field currently lacks standardized methodologies for detecting and analyzing these signals, limiting reproducibility and broader adoption of acoustic monitoring techniques.  
This work systematically compares three published event detection methods for airborne plant acoustic emissions: threshold-based detection with dual-microphone validation (Khait et al.), multi-band filtering with statistical thresholds (Bonisoli et al.), and spectrogram-based peak detection (Klaminder et al.). We implement each method in Python and evaluate their performance across multiple datasets, examining the trade-offs between computational efficiency, sensitivity, and false alarm rates. Key differences in filter design (FIR vs. IIR), signal processing approaches (time-domain vs. frequency-domain), and threshold selection strategies are analyzed.  
Our results provide practical guidance for researchers seeking to incorporate acoustic measurements into plant physiological studies. We make all code publicly available to reduce barriers to entry for those with limited signal processing experience. This work demonstrates how cross-disciplinary collaboration between physics, engineering, and plant biology can advance our understanding of plant biomechanics through innovative measurement approaches, while highlighting the need for community-driven standardization in this emerging field."
 
**Final:**
 
Acoustic emissions from plants—transient mechanical vibrations in the xylem due to water movement—have traditionally been recorded using standardised commercial equipment from civil engineering. Recent studies have begun using low-cost microphones designed for recording bats or mammal vocalisations, opening new experimental possibilities but introducing significant methodological variability. Different research groups now use more varied microphone types, recording conditions, and signal processing approaches, making cross-study comparisons difficult and reproducibility challenging.   We systematically evaluated three event detection methods from published articles. We used a published dataset to prototype the algorithms, then synthetic signals for calibration and finally recordings from barley and rush plants in controlled conditions. Our analysis reveals differences in sensitivity and specificity across methods, with some approaches being considerably more conservative than others.   The results demonstrate that combining information from multiple microphones with statistically-derived thresholds enables more reliable event detection at higher noise levels. The rates of acoustic emissions were, however, much lower than expected given the reports in the literature, which could have been the result of a lack of transpiration and photosynthetic activity during the recordings.   This work provides open-source scripts for more reproducible acoustic emission analysis, reducing technical barriers for laboratories without signal processing expertise or established analysis pipelines.
